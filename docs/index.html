Make the most of it

This house is meant to be enjoyed — the fireplaces, the grills, the hot tub, the steam shower, the patio. Please use them all and have a wonderful time.

The safety notes dotted through this guide aren’t here to put you off; they’re just so everyone has a great, worry-free stay. Skim them once, then relax and enjoy.

A few local ideas to get you started

  • Lamb chops on the Egg: grab lamb chops from Trader Joe’s and lump charcoal from The Butchery, bring the Big Green Egg up to about 600°F, rub the chops in olive oil and thyme, and grill on both sides until browned but not burnt — if a flame starts to develop, drop the upper lid on the Egg to settle it.
  • A morning out: breakfast at Plums or Breakfast Republic, both within walking distance. For something scenic, Driftwood Café in Laguna Beach sits right over the ocean and the sand.
  • Lunch: Zinque in Lido Marina Village — excellent French-infused food with a marina and harbour view, and lovely shopping right there.
  • An upscale evening: for something special, reserve dinner at The Dock.
  • A sunrise worth setting an alarm for: head to Dory’s Fish Market (by the Newport Pier) early for sunrise and fresh fish and shellfish straight off the boats. Come at 6 am sharp or the good stuff is gone — the Black Cod is the one to get.

Indoor fireplaces

There are two indoor gas fireplaces, lit by hand. The gas keys are in the main kitchen-island drawer.

To light one: insert the gas key into the valve, turn it to start the gas, then light it manually straight away. To turn it off, turn the key back until the gas is fully shut off.

Please take care with the fireplaces

  • Keep the gas keys out of reach of children.
  • Use caution when lighting — light right after the gas is on, and keep your hands and face back from the flame.
  • Stay clear of the fire and never leave it unattended; keep anything flammable well away.
  • When you’re finished, always make sure the gas is completely shut off.

Outdoor fire features

Out on the patio there’s a tabletop glass-enclosed fireplace and an open fire pit. Both run on gas and have their own keys — also in the main kitchen-island drawer (different from the indoor ones).

The tabletop fireplace is glass-enclosed and generally safe. The open fire pit needs more care:

Open fire pit

  • The flame is open and easy for children to reach — keep little ones well back, and never leave it on without an adult watching.
  • Don’t open the gas all the way — the flame can get very strong. Turn it on partway for a comfortable fire.
  • When you’re done, make sure the gas is fully shut off.

Charcoal grill — Big Green Egg

There’s a Large Big Green Egg (a ceramic charcoal grill and smoker) on the patio. It cooks beautifully once it’s up to temperature. Charcoal, the EGGniter (electric starter), and heat-proof gloves are [where].

Lighting it (with the EGGniter electric starter)

  1. Fill the firebox with natural lump charcoal (never briquettes, and never lighter fluid) to about the level of the fire ring.
  2. Open the bottom vent and the top vent fully.
  3. Plug in the EGGniter and press its tip into the charcoal. After 2–3 minutes the coals around it will glow and catch.
  4. Unplug it and carefully withdraw it, then rest it on a heatproof surface, well away from anything flammable, to cool — the tip stays scorching hot for a good while.
  5. Leave the dome open for about 10 minutes until the fire spreads, then close the dome.

Controlling the temperature

  • Airflow is the throttle: open the vents wider for hotter, narrow them for cooler.
  • Come up to your target slowly — the ceramic holds heat, and it’s far easier to raise the temperature than to bring it back down. Let it settle for 10–15 minutes before cooking.
  • For anything low-and-slow, fit the heat deflector (the ceramic plate) for indirect heat; remove it for direct grilling.

Shutting it down

  • When you’re finished, close both the top and bottom vents completely. Starving it of air puts the fire out on its own — no water needed. Leftover charcoal can be reused next time.

Always ‘burp’ before opening a hot dome

With the grill hot, opening the dome all at once can cause a flashback of flame. First crack the dome open just a couple of inches, pause a second or two to let air in gently, then open it fully — and always wear the heat-proof gloves.

Grill safety

  • The Egg and its metal bands get extremely hot — keep children and pets well back, and use the gloves.
  • Use it outdoors only, on its stand, well away from walls, railings, and anything flammable. Never move it indoors or under cover while lit (carbon-monoxide risk).
  • Never use lighter fluid — it damages the ceramic and taints the food.
  • Don’t leave it unattended while lit, and let it cool completely before covering.
  • The EGGniter tip gets red-hot — never touch it, keep the unit and its cord away from water, and rest it on a heatproof surface to cool fully before storing.

Gas grill — Lion 48-inch

Alongside the Egg there’s a Lion 48-inch stainless natural-gas grill, plumbed to the house gas line — no propane tank to deal with.

Lighting it

  1. Open the lid first — always light with the lid open.
  2. Make sure the gas supply valve is on [where the shut-off valve is].
  3. Turn the first burner knob to high and press the igniter (or hold a long lighter to the burner). It should catch within a few seconds.
  4. Once it’s lit, bring up the other burners one at a time, then preheat with the lid down for about 10–15 minutes.

If a burner doesn’t light within a few seconds, turn the knob off, wait five minutes for any gas to clear, then try again.

Cooking & shutting down

  • Adjust each burner knob to set hotter and cooler zones. [If equipped: the rotisserie/back and side burners light the same way.]
  • When you’re done, turn all burner knobs off, then turn off the gas at the supply valve. Brush the grates while they’re still warm, and cover the grill once it has cooled.

Gas grill safety

  • Never light it with the lid closed — gas can build up and flash back. Lid open, every time.
  • If a burner won’t light, shut it off and wait five minutes before retrying. If you smell gas, turn off the supply valve, leave the lid open to air out, and don’t light it (see the gas note in Safety & emergencies).
  • Stay with it while it’s on, watch for grease flare-ups, and keep children and pets back — the grates and surfaces get very hot.
  • Turn the gas off at the supply valve when you’re finished.

Hot tub — Sundance Spa

There’s a Sundance hot tub on the patio — set up and ready for you on arrival: clean, balanced, and warmed up.

Using it

  • Slide the cover off, and please put it back on after each use — it keeps the water clean, warm, and safe.
  • Use the control panel to check the temperature and switch the jets on. [Any specifics about your panel.]
  • A comfortable soak is around 15–20 minutes.

Keeping the water balanced

We keep basic spa chemicals and test strips stocked [where]. Looking after the water during your stay is up to you — use the strips as your guide and adjust to taste. (There are plenty of quick Sundance water-care guides online if you’d like a refresher.)

If the water looks or smells off

Cloudy, foamy, a strong smell, or you just can’t get it right? Stop using it and message us rather than adding more chemicals — and please don’t drain or refill the tub; leave that to us.

Handling spa chemicals safely

Keep all chemicals out of reach of children, never mix them together, always add chemical to water (never water to chemical), and wash your hands afterwards. Store them sealed and dry.

Hot tub safety — please read

  • Small children can drown in a hot tub — even in shallow water, and in moments. Constant, hands-on supervision is essential whenever children are anywhere near it. Young children shouldn’t use it at all, and keep the cover on and latched when it’s not in use.
  • Don’t use it if you’re pregnant, have heart trouble, high or low blood pressure, or any condition affected by heat — check with your doctor if you’re unsure. Never use it after alcohol or drugs.
  • Keep the water no hotter than 104°F (40°C), limit soaks to about 15–20 minutes, stay hydrated, and get out at once if you feel dizzy, drowsy, or unwell.
  • Keep long hair, hands, and feet clear of the suction and drains.
  • No glass near the tub, and step carefully — the surround gets slippery.
  • Don’t use the hot tub during a thunderstorm.
  • Keep your head above water — don’t submerge or hold your breath.
  • Shower before getting in, and skip the tub if you have open wounds or are feeling unwell.
  • Electrical safety: keep phones, speakers, and chargers away from the water. The hot tub’s power shut-off is on the wall near the Lion grill — use it to cut power in an emergency or before any upkeep.

Steam shower (master bath)

The master bathroom has a steam shower (a 12 kW steam system). You’re welcome to use it at your own risk — please read the safety notes below first.

Using it

  1. With the shower door closed, use the control panel to set your time and temperature, then start it.
  2. Give it a few minutes to build up; keep the door closed to hold the steam in.
  3. Keep sessions short — 15 minutes is plenty. It times out on its own, or switch it off when you’re done.

Steam shower — please read before using

  • Use the steam shower at your own risk.
  • Don’t use it if you’re pregnant, have heart trouble, high or low blood pressure, or any condition affected by heat — check with your doctor if you’re unsure.
  • It’s not suitable for young children or anyone frail or impaired, and no one in those groups should use it unattended. Don’t use it after alcohol.
  • The steam outlet and surfaces get very hot — keep clear of the steam head to avoid burns, and never drape towels or clothing over it.
  • Stay hydrated, and get out right away if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or unwell.

Slippery when wet

Steam leaves floors and surfaces wet and slippery. Step carefully getting in and out, use the bath mat, and wipe down the floor afterwards so no one slips. Leave the door open to let it dry out.

It may bring out the tar

The steam will likely amplify the tar that can seep from the grout line (see Good to know — quirks & pitfalls). After a steam session, give any spots a quick wipe with the Goo Gone and paper towels under the left sink.

The espresso machine

The kitchen has a built-in Thermador espresso machine. It slides out of the cabinetry on a handle at the bottom — pull it out whenever you need to fill the beans or water, or empty the trays.

Beans

  • Whole beans only. The bean compartment is at the right rear, labelled.
  • Beans can occasionally get stuck — if the grinder sounds like it’s running empty, give them a quick stir so they keep feeding.

Water

  • The water tank is on the left. Lift it out, fill it with water, and slide it back in.

Making a coffee

Select the coffee dial — Espresso, Coffee, and so on — then press “Café” to brew.

If it says “Empty Trays”

Slide the machine out and remove the tray and clean it. Importantly, brush the metal sensors at the back — they need to be clean for the machine to register the tray as empty again.

If you see “Error 8”

This usually clears with a quick reset:

  1. Slide the machine out, find the red on/off button, and turn it off for 10 seconds.
  2. Remove the tray and clean it again, then put it back.
  3. Turn the red button back on, switch Standby on, and wait for the machine to heat up.
  4. Repeat as needed until Error 8 clears.

Red “Calc” light

You can safely ignore the red “Calc” light.

Good to know — quirks & pitfalls

Every home has a few quirks. Here’s a heads-up on ours so nothing catches you off guard.

Range: the two left burners are out of service

The kitchen has a 6-burner Thermador range with a grill. The two left burners aren’t working at the moment — we’re waiting on a back-ordered control module, and their knobs are labelled “Do not use.” The other four burners and the grill work as normal.

Upstairs tub: keep the water in the tub

The freestanding master tub upstairs has a handheld shower. Please keep the spray low and pointed into the tub, and wipe up any splashes — water that reaches the floor can seep down and damage the kitchen ceiling below.

Main shower: occasional tar at a grout line

You may spot a little dark tar seeping from a grout line in the main shower. It’s harmless — an in-progress repair we couldn’t finish before your stay. Just wipe it away with the Goo Gone and paper towels under the left sink.

Main shower: keep an eye on the drain

The main shower drains slowly when hair builds up — you may hear the drain click. Watch the water while you shower; if it starts to pool, lift the drain cover and clear the strainer. If that doesn’t fix it, message us right away and we’ll sort it out.

Just a quick safety round

Two minutes before you head out, so nothing’s left running:

  • Turn off all the gas — the indoor fireplaces, the fire pit and tabletop fireplace, and the Lion grill at its supply valve. Make sure the Big Green Egg’s vents are closed.
  • Switch off the stove and oven, and turn off the steam shower.
  • Turn off the taps and check nothing is left running.

  • Close and lock the windows and doors, including the patio door, and pull the front door firmly shut behind you (the keypad locks it).
  • Take your belongings — one last look in the master bath, the closets, and out on the patio.

Safety & emergencies

In an emergency, your safety comes first — call the emergency number, then let the host know when you can.

Security cameras

For your safety and security, there are exterior cameras at the entryway and the patio. There are no cameras inside the house — your privacy indoors is fully respected.

  • Emergency services: dial 911.
  • Nearest ER: Hoag Hospital, Newport Beach — 1 Hoag Dr (open 24 hours, ~2 mi).
  • Urgent care (non-emergency): Exer Urgent Care, 235 E 17th St, Costa Mesa — open 8am–8pm.
  • Electrical panel / breakers: on the east side of the house, by the garbage.
  • Water shut-off valve: east side of the house, in the garbage section — turn clockwise to stop the water.
  • Gas shut-off: on the west side of the house, behind the fireplace (reachable from the backyard only) — look for the red handle.
  • Hot tub power shut-off: on the wall near the Lion grill.
  • Fire extinguishers: throughout the house — see the panel below.
  • First-aid kit: under the kitchen sink, next to the fire extinguisher.
  • Smoke & carbon-monoxide alarms: a CO alarm in the downstairs hallway, and smoke alarms in every bedroom — if one chirps, it’s usually the battery; let us know.
  • Two ways out: in a hurry, the front door and the patio door at the back.

Fire extinguishers — know where they are

There are eight fire extinguishers throughout the house. Please familiarise yourself with the locations, and make sure everyone in your group — children included — knows where they are and how to use one.

  • Upstairs: the kids’ bathroom and the master bathroom — both under the sink.
  • Downstairs: under the bathroom sink, the laundry sink, in the pantry, under the bar sink, and under the kitchen sink.
  • Outdoors: under the Big Green Egg.

The one under the kitchen sink is a special kitchen extinguisher — use that one for kitchen or grease fires.

If you smell gas

Don’t switch anything electrical on or off. Open the windows, leave the house, and call the emergency number from outside.

Fire & carbon monoxide

With gas appliances and several fireplaces here, a few essentials: carbon monoxide is invisible and odourless, so never block the smoke or CO alarms, and never bring the fire pit, tabletop fireplace, a BBQ, or any open flame indoors or into an enclosed space. Don’t leave the gas range or a lit fireplace unattended, and keep anything flammable well clear of the flames.

If there’s a fire

Get everyone out first, then call 911 from outside — don’t go back in for belongings. Only use the extinguisher on a small, contained fire that hasn’t started to spread.

Earthquakes

Southern California gets the occasional tremor. If the ground shakes, Drop, Cover, and Hold On under a sturdy table, away from windows and the fireplaces. Afterwards, check for the smell of gas and shut it off if you notice any.

Travelling with little ones

Please keep children supervised near the open fire pit and the stairs, store the gas keys out of reach, and take care with water around the upstairs tub (see the quirks).
